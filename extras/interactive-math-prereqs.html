01

Language Models

A language model learns the joint probability of a token sequence autoregressively — each prediction depends only on the tokens before it. Everything below is what happens between "tokens in" and "loss out."

Autoregressive factorization

Given x = (x₁,…,x_T), the model factorizes the sequence probability as a chain rule: P_θ(x) = ∏ₜ P_θ(xₜ | x<ₜ). Click a token to see its conditional term — and notice that later tokens are invisible to it.

Green = conditioning context (already seen). Highlighted = the token being predicted. Dim = not yet visible — this is the causal constraint in action.

Softmax & temperature

The LM head produces one logit per vocabulary token; softmax turns logits into a probability distribution: softmax(z/T)ᵢ = e^(zᵢ/T) / Σⱼ e^(zⱼ/T). Drag the logits or temperature and watch the distribution sharpen or flatten.

Higher T → flatter, more diverse distribution. Lower T → sharper, closer to argmax (greedy).

Anatomy of one LM training example

The same pipeline, one line at a time: input_ids → logits → shift → log_softmax → gather → mask & sum. Step through it and watch the tensor shapes change — B = batch, L = sequence length, V = vocab size.

Three masks

Silent bugs live here. The causal mask makes the model autoregressive, the padding mask ignores filler tokens, and the completion mask decides which tokens actually contribute to the loss. Switch tabs to see each one act on the same sequence.

Cross-entropy / negative log-likelihood

The true next-token label is one-hot, so cross-entropy collapses to a single term: H(q,P_θ) = −log P_θ(xₜ | x<ₜ). Drag the predicted probability of the true token and watch the loss curve −log(p).

As p → 1, loss → 0 (confident & correct). As p → 0, loss → ∞ (confident & wrong is punished hard).

02

Optimization & Fine-tuning

Training repeats one step: θ ← θ − η ∇_θ L(θ). What differs across pretraining, mid-training, and SFT is masking, sampling, and data — not the optimizer.

Gradient descent

Click anywhere on the curve to place θ, then step downhill. The dashed line shows the local gradient — descent moves opposite to it. Try a learning rate that's too large and watch it overshoot.

Pretraining → mid-training → SFT

The boundary at the end of pretraining is fuzzy but important: it's where the loss mask changes from "every token" to "only the response."

03

Probability Essentials

KL divergence is the central object of alignment — it's how the RLHF KL penalty and the DPO loss are both derived. Sigmoid turns score differences into preference probabilities.

Entropy, cross-entropy & KL divergence

One identity ties them together: H(p,q) = H(p) + D_KL(p‖q). Drag the weights for two distributions p and q over 5 outcomes and watch all three quantities update live.

Estimating KL by Monte Carlo

Over sequences, the KL sum is intractable — but KL is an expectation, so we estimate it from samples. The naive average (k₁) is unbiased but can go negative; RLHF's k₃ estimator is always ≥ 0 and lower-variance. Draw samples from p and watch both estimators converge to the true KL.

Sigmoid & the Bradley–Terry model

A preference is binary, so a reward difference is squeezed through a sigmoid: P(y_w ≻ y_l) = σ(r(x,y_w) − r(x,y_l)). Only the relative difference matters. Drag Δr and watch the preference probability and the implied loss −log σ(Δr).

Δr = 0 → toss-up (P = 0.5). Large positive Δr → near-certain preference for y_w.

04

Reinforcement Learning Framing

An LM is an MDP in disguise: the policy is the next-token distribution, the action is the next token, and the reward is usually one scalar at the very end. The last panel goes one step past the prereq deck into PPO — the algorithm that actually updates that policy.

MDP conceptLanguage model

The goal of RL: sparse, delayed reward

J(θ) = E_{y~π_θ}[R(x,y)] — maximize expected reward. A preference label is one scalar at the end of a response: sparse, delayed, with no token-level target. The policy gradient distributes that one number's credit across every token's log-probability.

Each bar is a token's sampled log-probability. The single reward at the end is the only training signal — there's no per-token ground truth.

PPO: the clipped policy update

The bare policy gradient above is high-variance and unstable — one big step off the data you sampled can wreck the policy. PPO works off the probability ratio r = π_θ(a) / π_old(a) and clips it so a single update can't move too far. The surrogate is min( r·A , clip(r, 1−ε, 1+ε)·A ), where A is the advantage (how much better than the baseline). Drag the ratio and watch the objective flatten the moment you leave the trust region.
