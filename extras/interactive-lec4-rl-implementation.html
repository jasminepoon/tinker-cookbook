Foundations — start here

PPO, GRPO and friends look like alphabet soup, but the moving parts are three everyday operations: a ratio (how much bigger one number is than another), clipping (refusing to let a number leave a safe range), and standardizing (re-scaling a batch of numbers to a common mean and spread). Get these three and the algorithms read like plumbing.

Ratios: "how many times as likely?"

These methods compare the new policy to the old one by dividing their probabilities. A ratio of 1 means "no change," 1.5 means "50% more likely now," 0.5 means "half as likely." It's just division — but it's the quantity every clipped objective acts on.

probability ratio. : the update made this action more likely; : less likely.

Clipping: capping a value to a safe range

Clipping (a.k.a. clamping) is "don't let this number go below a floor or above a ceiling." Inside the range it passes through unchanged; outside, it sticks to the nearest edge. PPO clips the ratio so a single update can't lunge too far from the data it was measured on.

the clip width (often 0.2). The allowed band is ; beyond it the gradient goes flat, so there's no reward for moving further.

Mean & standard deviation: standardizing a batch

The mean is the average; the standard deviation is the typical distance from that average (the spread). GRPO takes a group of rewards, subtracts the mean (so they center on 0) and divides by the std (so their scale is comparable) — turning raw scores into advantages that say "better or worse than the group, and by how much."

the group's average reward — the new "zero point."
the spread; dividing by it makes advantages comparable across easy and hard prompts.

The policy-gradient family

Every method in this lecture minimizes the same loss skeleton — a sum of log-probs weighted by an advantage . They differ only in what is (the baseline / advantage) and how updates are bounded (clipping). Climb the ladder and see exactly what each algorithm adds.

GRPO & group-relative advantages

GRPO throws away the value function and GAE entirely. For each prompt it samples a group of completions and computes advantages purely by comparing siblings: . Same clipped loss as PPO, one fewer model in memory.

Group-relative advantages (the core GRPO idea)

Sample a group of responses for one prompt, give them toy rewards, then center within the group: subtract the group mean and divide by the group std. The baseline is the group itself — better-than-average siblings get positive advantage, worse-than-average get negative. Watch the std-collapse warning when all rewards are equal (the NaN trap).

KL penalty placement: in-reward vs in-loss

Same goal — constrain drift from the reference policy — two placements. PPO folds into the per-token reward before GAE, so KL flows through advantage estimation. GRPO adds as a separate loss term, leaving advantages untouched. Toggle and see what the per-token training signal becomes.

In practice · Either placement is the same brake on over-optimization — what matters operationally is that β is tuned and monitored at all; a silently-zeroed KL term is a common way runs hack their reward without anyone noticing until evals drop.
04

Memory budget & loss aggregation

Two practical realities. PPO juggles four models in memory; GRPO drops one. And the seemingly cosmetic choice of how you reduce per-token losses to a scalar changes training dynamics — biasing the model toward short or verbose answers.

The four models in memory (and how GRPO drops one)

PPO keeps the policy (trained), a learned value/critic, a frozen reference (KL anchor), and a frozen reward model resident at once. For a 7B model in fp16 that's ~14 GB each. Toggle to GRPO and the value network vanishes — no critic, no GAE, no value loss, ~one fewer copy. Pick a model size and see the total weight footprint.

Loss aggregation: per-sequence vs per-token vs fixed-length

Same gradient , three ways to reduce it to a batch scalar. Per-sequence (GRPO) gives each sequence equal weight → short sequences get bigger per-token gradients.
