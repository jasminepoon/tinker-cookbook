01

The Promise of DPO

Classic RLHF has three moving parts: collect preference pairs, train a reward model rφ(x,y), then optimize the policy against it with RL (e.g. PPO) under a KL penalty. DPO collapses steps 2 and 3 into one supervised-style loss. The key realization we will prove: the optimal RLHF policy and the reward model are two views of the same object — know one, and you know the other in closed form.

Where DPO sits: collapsing the pipeline

RLHF is reward-model-then-RL. DPO trains the policy directly on preference pairs. Toggle between the two pipelines to see which stages each one needs — DPO drops the separate reward model and the online RL loop entirely.

The Derivation, Step by Step

The whole derivation runs in four parts: (P1) solve the KL-regularized RLHF objective for its optimal policy via the partition function; (P2) invert that to write the reward in terms of the policy; (P3) plug it into the Bradley–Terry preference model so the partition function cancels; (P4) read off the DPO loss. 03

The Optimal Policy

The RLHF objective's closed-form solution is π*(y|x) = (1/Z(x)) · πref(y|x) · exp(r(x,y)/β): take the reference policy and reweight each response by er/β, then renormalize with the partition function Z(x). β controls how aggressive that reweighting is.

How β morphs πref into the reward-tilted optimum

A small set of candidate responses, each with a reference probability and a reward. The green bars are πref; the bright bars are π*. Slide β: as β→∞ the KL penalty dominates and π* collapses back onto πref; as β→0 the reward dominates and π* becomes greedy, piling all mass on the highest-reward response. Edit the rewards to see the tilt change.

04

The Implicit Reward

Inverting the optimal policy gives r*(x,y) = β·log(π*(y|x)/πref(y|x)) + β·log Z(x) — "your language model is secretly a reward model." The reward is just β times the log-ratio of the policy against the reference (plus a per-prompt constant β log Z that cancels in any preference comparison).

Reading the reward off the log-ratio

Drag the policy and reference log-probabilities for a response, and β. The implied reward is β·(log πθ − log πref). When the policy assigns the response more mass than the reference, the implicit reward is positive; less mass, negative. The β log Z term is a prompt-level offset — toggle it to see it shift every reward by the same amount.

Only the log-ratio is learnable; log Z(x) is the same for every response to a given prompt, so it never affects which response is preferred.

05

The DPO Loss

Substituting the implicit reward into Bradley–Terry, the partition function cancels and the preference becomes a sigmoid of a difference of log-ratios. Minimizing the negative log-likelihood of the observed preferences gives the DPO loss: L = −log σ( β·[ log(πθ(yw)/πref(yw)) − log(πθ(yl)/πref(yl)) ] ).

The DPO loss, live

Four log-probabilities (policy and reference, for the chosen yw and rejected yl) plus β. The loss rewards widening the margin: pushing the chosen log-ratio above the rejected one relative to the reference. Watch the implicit reward margin and loss respond. Notice that the loss only sees the margin — not the absolute log-probs (the likelihood-displacement risk).

06

The Gradient: how DPO learns

The DPO gradient is ∇L = −β·E[ w·(∇log πθ(yw) − ∇log πθ(yl)) ] with weight w = σ(r̂θ(yl) − r̂θ(yw)). The bracket raises the likelihood of the chosen response and lowers the rejected one; the scalar weight w ∈ (0,1) is larger exactly when the model currently misranks the pair — the σ' weighting up-weights the wrong examples.

The "how-wrong" weight w

Slide the implicit-reward margin Δ = r̂θ(yw) − r̂θ(yl) (positive = correctly ranked). The gradient weight is w = σ(−Δ). When the pair is badly misranked (Δ ≪ 0), w → 1 and the step is large; when the model already ranks the pair confidently right (Δ ≫ 0), w → 0 and the example is nearly ignored. This is the same σ' self-weighting as in logistic regression.

DPO vs. PPO / online RL

DPO is offline: it steps directly to the optimum implied by a fixed dataset and the chosen β, so its final KL distance from the reference is static. Online RL (PPO) generates fresh completions, scores them with a reward model, and can explore — often a higher peak, at much more compute and complexity. Click a row to expand the contrast.

07

In Practice

β trades correct ordering against drift from the reference, and the loss is genuinely a few lines of code. A subtle failure mode — likelihood displacement — is worth seeing directly.

Likelihood displacement: the chosen probability can fall

The loss only cares about the margin. So the model can lower the loss by pushing the rejected log-prob down faster than the chosen — even while the chosen log-prob also falls. Drag the per-step changes to each policy log-prob and watch: the margin can widen (loss drops) while the chosen probability drifts downward. Practitioners sometimes add an SFT term on the chosen response to counter this.

The whole loss, in code

No generation during training, no separate reward model. πref is frozen, so its log-probs can be precomputed and cached to cut peak memory roughly in half. Step through to see how the four cached log-probs become the loss.
