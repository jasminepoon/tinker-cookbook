Foundations — start here

The DPO derivation chains together three school-level tools: logs and exponentials (which undo each other), the sigmoid (a score gap → a probability), and a ratio of two models' probabilities. If those three feel shaky, read this first — the rest of the page is just bookkeeping with them.

Logs and exponentials undo each other

DPO constantly flips between a probability and its log. turns a score into a positive number; turns it back. They're mirror images across the line — that reversibility is what lets the derivation rewrite the reward in terms of the policy and back again.

The sigmoid, and the −log on top of it

The DPO loss is — exactly the same two-step move as a preference model. The sigmoid turns a margin into a probability between 0 and 1; the −log turns "probability you got the preference right" into a loss that's gentle when you're right and steep when you're wrong.

A ratio of two models: how much more the policy likes an answer

DPO never uses a separate reward network. The "implicit reward" is just how much more probability the trained policy puts on an answer than the frozen reference does. Divide the two probabilities; take the log. Above 1 (positive log) the policy prefers it more than the reference; below 1, less.

01

The Promise of DPO

Classic RLHF has three moving parts: collect preference pairs, train a reward model , then optimize the policy against it with RL (e.g. PPO) under a KL penalty. DPO collapses steps 2 and 3 into one supervised-style loss. The key realization we will prove: the optimal RLHF policy and the reward model are two views of the same object — know one, and you know the other in closed form.

Where DPO sits: collapsing the pipeline

RLHF is reward-model-then-RL. DPO trains the policy directly on preference pairs. Toggle between the two pipelines to see which stages each one needs — DPO drops the separate reward model and the online RL loop entirely.

The Derivation, Step by Step

The Implicit Reward

Inverting the optimal policy — "your language model is secretly a reward model." The reward is just times the log-ratio of the policy against the reference, plus a per-prompt constant that cancels in any preference comparison.

05

The DPO Loss

Substituting the implicit reward into Bradley–Terry, the partition function cancels and the preference becomes a sigmoid of a difference of log-ratios. Minimizing the negative log-likelihood of the observed preferences gives the DPO loss.

The Gradient: how DPO learns

The DPO gradient raises the likelihood of the chosen response and lowers the rejected one; the scalar weight is larger exactly when the model currently misranks the pair — the weighting up-weights the wrong examples.

DPO vs. PPO / online RL

DPO is offline: it steps directly to the optimum implied by a fixed dataset and the chosen β, so its final KL distance from the reference is static. Online RL (PPO) generates fresh completions, scores them with a reward model, and can explore — often a higher peak, at much more compute and complexity. Click a row to expand the contrast.

07

In Practice

β trades correct ordering against drift from the reference, and the loss is genuinely a few lines of code. A subtle failure mode — likelihood displacement — is worth seeing directly.

Likelihood displacement: the chosen probability can fall

The loss only cares about the margin. So the model can lower the loss by pushing the rejected log-prob down faster than the chosen — even while the chosen log-prob also falls. Drag the per-step changes to each policy log-prob and watch: the margin can widen (loss drops) while the chosen probability drifts downward. Practitioners sometimes add an SFT term on the chosen response to counter this.

In practice · DPO has no explicit reward model to audit, but it is not immune to bad preference data — the margin can "look" fine while the chosen response's own likelihood quietly degrades. The same data QA discipline (held-out checks, not just train loss) still applies.
