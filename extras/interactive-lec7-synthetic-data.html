Foundations — start here

This page is about one model teaching another. The core math is comparing two probability distributions — the teacher's answer and the student's — and measuring the gap with KL divergence. Two intuitions carry the whole page: "soft" targets say more than a single right answer, and matching a distribution can be done from the teacher's side (forward KL) or the student's side (reverse KL).

"Soft" targets carry more information than one right answer

A normal training label is one-hot: "the next word is cat, everything else is wrong." A teacher model instead hands over a full distribution — cat 60%, kitten 25%, dog 10%… That extra shape tells the student how the words relate, which is why distillation transfers so much more than plain imitation.

KL divergence: the gap between two distributions

KL divergence is one number for "how different is distribution from distribution ?" It's when they match exactly and grows as they diverge. Distillation trains the student by driving this gap toward zero.

two distributions being compared (e.g. teacher and student next-word probabilities).

Forward vs reverse: which side you match from

KL is asymmetric — — and that asymmetry has a personality. Forward KL (average over the teacher's data) makes the student "mass-covering": it tries to put some probability everywhere the teacher does. Reverse KL (average over the student's own samples) makes it "mode-seeking": it picks one good mode and commits. On-policy distillation later on is exactly the reverse-KL choice.

Distillation with synthetic data

Distillation transfers capability from a stronger teacher to a student. Classic knowledge distillation matches the teacher's soft next-token distribution rather than one-hot targets. Two flavours: match every token's full distribution (word-level), or train on whole teacher-generated sequences (sequence-level, which reduces to SFT).

Word-level vs sequence-level distillation

Word-KD matches the teacher's full vocabulary distribution at every position — soft labels, cost . The whole point of "on-policy". Offline KD / SFT minimizes forward KL — the expectation is over the teacher, so it is mass-covering: the student must cover everything the teacher might say. The path to on-policy distillation

Offline KD trains on teacher trajectories, but at test time the student rolls out under its own policy — a train/test state mismatch called exposure bias. On-policy distillation (the DAgger move) samples from the student and lets the teacher score its own visited states, dropping compounding error from to . 03

AI feedback & Constitutional AI

RLAIF replaces the human-data step with AI: AI preference labels cost <$0.01/prompt vs $1–$10+ for humans. Constitutional AI (CAI RLAIF) was the earliest large-scale synthetic-RLHF method: a model critiques and revises its own answers against a written constitution (Stage 1 → SFT), then AI preference labels drive RL (Stage 2). Rubrics extend this to prompt-specific, near-verifiable criteria.

Constitutional AI: the critique → revise loop

Stage 1 of CAI. The model drafts a response, samples a principle from the constitution, critiques its latest draft against that principle, and revises. Repeating yields one SFT point — the refined final answer. Pick a principle and step the loop; watch the draft get progressively safer/more aligned.

The bias–variance tradeoff: human vs AI feedback

A colloquial rule of thumb from the early RLHF-vs-RLAIF debates. Human preference data is high-noise, low-bias: each label is wobbly, but the signal is unbiased and reliable once aggregated. AI feedback is low-noise, high-bias: cheap and consistent, but with systematic errors baked in (e.g. self-preference bias). Mix them: route hard points to humans, most to AI. Rubrics: prompt-specific AI feedback

For prompts with no single right answer, score completions against tagged criteria. [Hard Rule] items are atomic must-pass checks; [Principle] items are softer quality criteria. The total rubric score sums the satisfied items' weights — RL then updates toward the best-scoring answers. Toggle which criteria a candidate answer satisfies and watch the score and pass/fail verdict update.

Click a criterion to toggle whether this candidate answer satisfies it. Hard rules must all pass for a valid answer; principles add graded quality on top.

In practice · Rubrics are the practical middle ground between a binary verifier and a vague learned reward model: for domains without one checkable answer, hand-written, auditable criteria give RL a reward signal that's at least partially inspectable and harder to silently game.
