01

Language Models & Their History

A language model assigns probabilities to text. Words are broken into tokens; given the previous tokens it predicts the next one, and repeating that — autoregressively — produces a completion one step at a time. Modern models have billions to trillions of parameters and are largely downstream of the 2017 Transformer.

From Base Model to Chatbot

After pretraining we have "a glorified autocomplete model." Post-training reshapes how it responds. The deck breaks the change into stages — pretraining builds world knowledge, SFT teaches the answer format, preference tuning/RLHF gives response-level feedback, and RLVR sharpens verifiable skills.

03

Classical Reinforcement Learning

RLHF inherits its language from classical RL, written as a Markov Decision Process (𝒮, 𝒜, P, r, γ). The agent observes a state, its policy picks an action, the environment returns a reward and a next state; the goal is to maximize cumulative return J(π) = E[Σₜ γᵗ r(sₜ,aₜ)].

The agent–environment loop (Thermostat)

The deck's thermostat example. State = room temperature; action = heater on/off; reward = high when near the target. The policy here is: heater on if temperature < 70°F, else off. Step the loop and watch state → policy → action → reward → next state with live numbers, and the return accumulate.

Policy: π(on | s) = 1 if s < 70°F else 0. Reward r(s) = max(0, 1 − |s − target|/10). The heater warms the room ~+3°F when on; it cools ~−2°F when off.

Why RLHF? Rewards you can't write down

In CartPole the reward is a known formula (+1 per upright step). But it's easy to judge which response is better and hard to specify "helpful" as an equation. RLHF (and its predecessors — TAMER 2008, Christiano 2017's Atari/backflip preferences, Ziegler 2019 on LMs) optimizes behavior we can evaluate even when we can't specify the reward. Drag the two backflip scores and pick the better one.

There's no closed-form reward for "a good backflip" — so a human just picks the better clip. That single bit of preference becomes the training signal (Christiano et al. 2017).

04

InstructGPT's 3-Step RLHF Recipe

InstructGPT (Ouyang et al. 2022) made the canonical three-stage recipe standard: instruction fine-tuning, reward modeling, then RL against the reward model. OpenAI hinted the original ChatGPT used exactly this. It became the intellectual template for modern post-training.

Reward modeling & the Bradley–Terry preview

An annotator sees two responses to a prompt and picks the winner. The reward model r_φ is trained so the chosen response scores higher: P(y_w ≻ y_l) = σ(r_φ(x,y_w) − r_φ(x,y_l)), minimizing −log σ(Δr). The reward is literally the model's predicted probability that a piece of text is the "winning" completion. Drag the reward gap Δr and watch the preference probability and loss respond.

Only the difference Δr = r_w − r_l matters. Δr = 0 → toss-up (P = 0.5). Large positive Δr → near-certain preference for the winner, and the RM loss → 0.

05

The RLHF Objective, Unpacked

Step 3 brings it together: sample prompts, generate completions, score them with the reward model, and update the policy — but with a KL penalty keeping the policy close to the reference (SFT) model. The whole thing is one objective with a knob, β, controlling the tradeoff.

J(θ) = E[ r(x,y) ] − β · D_KL(π‖π_ref)

The tug-of-war: the first term pulls the policy toward high reward; the second pulls it back toward the reference model. Drag the policy's chosen reward and the KL distance from the reference, then turn β to see who wins. The deck's annotation: "maximize the reward, but don't change the model too much."

Over-optimization: the reward is a proxy

The reward model is only correlated with real quality (Goodhart's Law). Push the policy too far from the reference and the proxy reward keeps climbing while true quality turns over — reward hacking, verbosity, sycophancy. β is the main defense. Drag the optimization pressure (how far the policy drifts) and watch proxy vs. true reward diverge.

06

The Preference-Tuning Landscape

Rejection sampling, online RL (PPO), and DPO all optimize the same underlying objective — they differ in how they move the policy toward higher-reward completions. DPO even eliminates the separate reward model by training an implicit one directly on preferences.

DPO: skip the reward model

DPO derives the gradient toward the optimal policy directly, training on preferred (y_w) vs. rejected (y_l) pairs. Its loss reuses the same Bradley–Terry sigmoid, but on a policy-vs-reference log-ratio: −log σ( β·log[π_θ(y_w)/π_ref(y_w)] − β·log[π_θ(y_l)/π_ref(y_l)] ). Drag the policy's log-probs and watch the implicit reward margin and loss.

The implicit reward is β·log[π_θ/π_ref]. Raising y_w's log-ratio above y_l's drives the loss down — exactly the contrastive push of preference tuning, no reward model required.
