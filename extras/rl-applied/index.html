00

The throughline — one pipeline, three weeks

RLHF is one arc, not a pile of topics: collect preferences → train a reward model that turns those preferences into a score → train a policy that maximizes that score. This lab builds exactly that arc on a real research problem — your α-sweep activation-steering images and your own preference judgments over them. Week 1 forges the engine (REINFORCE by hand); Weeks 2 and 3 are the two halves of the arc. Read this diagram once; every week below is a zoom-in on one box.

The whole lab on one screen

The same three ideas recur every week: a policy is a distribution you sample from, you nudge it with log-prob × advantage, and a baseline keeps that nudge low-variance. Week 1 teaches those ideas in the simplest possible world; Week 3 reuses your Week-1 code almost verbatim to drive the reward model you build in Week 2.

the RLHF arc (preferences → RM → policy) the engine & the failure mode you watch for
Why this order Week 1 is standalone practice: the same update powers Week 3. Weeks 2→3 are the actual pipeline: preferences become a reward model, then a policy chases that reward. The dashed orange loop is where you'll go looking for reward hacking once the loop closes.

01

Week 1 — REINFORCE from scratch

Close the theory→code gap. No framework, no autograd: for a softmax policy the gradient of has a closed form, so you type the policy-gradient estimator literally. When you later read a torch loss like -(logprobs * advantages).mean(), you'll know exactly what it expands to. Two exercises: a bandit, then a gridworld.

A policy is a distribution — and the gradient has a closed form

Parameters become action probabilities through softmax; you sample an action, see a reward, and push probability toward actions that did better than a baseline. The whole update is one line, and for softmax the gradient term is just — no autograd needed.

the policy's parameters — one number per (state, action), turned into probabilities by softmax
learning rate — how big a step you take
return: the (discounted) reward earned from time onward — how good this action turned out
baseline: a reference level (running mean, or a learned ) subtracted to cut variance
probability the policy assigns to action in state
The gridworld twist The second exercise adds state: , returns-to-go for credit assignment, and a learned baseline — the tabular ancestor of PPO's value head. Without the baseline the run often collapses (every return is negative, so every action taken gets pushed down): the visceral version of "advantage normalization matters."
Grounds lec3 (policy gradients — the log-derivative trick and baselines) and lec4 (RL implementation — REINFORCE → RLOO → PPO → GRPO). The offset in the bandit's arm means is deliberate: subtracting it is exactly what GRPO's group-mean baseline does.
Do this — week1_reinforce/
  • README.md — the two exercises, in order
  • bandit.py — 3 TODOs: softmax sampling, log-prob×advantage, running-mean baseline (solution: bandit_solution.py)
  • gridworld.py — 4 TODOs: state-conditioned sampling, returns-to-go, tabular PG update, learned (solution: gridworld_solution.py)

  • You can state from your own logs why no-baseline gradient variance is ~5–20× larger with a big reward offset.
  • Gridworld episode length converges to the optimal 8 with the baseline — and you can explain why the no-baseline run collapses instead.
02

Week 2 — a Bradley–Terry reward model on your data

The "RM" stage of RLHF, in miniature: your pairwise judgments become a scalar scorer. The featurizer is a frozen CLIP image embedding; the only trained part is a single linear head — exactly analogous to the scalar head on a frozen/finetuned LLM backbone. This turns "I prefer this image" into a number a policy can optimize.

Pairwise preferences → a scalar score (Bradley–Terry)

You never label an image with an absolute score — you only say "A beats B." Bradley–Terry converts those comparisons into scores by fitting them so that the gap between winner and loser, squashed through a sigmoid, matches how often the winner won. Minimizing is the same loss you saw in reward modeling and DPO.

frozen CLIP embedding of an image — the fixed featurizer
the linear head you train — the only learned parameters
scalar reward score for an image,
sigmoid: maps the score gap to a win-probability in (0, 1)
Scores are per-context Same-context pairs identify scores only up to a per-context constant — the RM is meaningful within a prompt, not across prompts (correlation >0.85 with hidden quality within a context). LLM reward models have the identical property; it's why RLHF advantages are computed relative to other samples of the same prompt.
Grounds the reward-modeling lecture and lec2 (IFT/RM/rejection), lec8 (preferences). Same shape as DPO in lec6 — you'll see that rhyme again in Week 4.
Do this — week2_reward_model/
  • README.md — synthetic first, then your real α-sweep images
  • python3 make_pairs.py --synthetic → builds embeddings + pairs (prints the rater-consistency ceiling)
  • reward_model.py — 2 TODOs: the BT loss, its gradient, plus the accuracy eval (solution: reward_model_solution.py)
  • Synthetic: val accuracy ≈ 79% — above chance (50%), near the printed ~84% ceiling; RM score correlates >0.85 with hidden quality within each context.
  • Real: RM trained on ~80% of your judgments, accuracy reported on the held-out 20%.
  • Grant milestone: a number — "a CLIP + linear head agrees with my held-out human preference judgments X% of the time."
03

Week 3 — close the loop: a policy that picks α

The "PPO stage," stripped to its skeleton. The environment is one step — pick α, get a reward, done — so this is a contextual bandit, which is exactly how RLHF is usually framed (one action = one full response). Your Week-1 bandit code returns almost unchanged, with one twist each TODO: state-conditioned sampling and a per-context baseline (the tabular version of GRPO's group-relative advantage).

Contextual bandit REINFORCE against your reward model

For each context (one of your prompts) the policy samples an α from a 9-value grid; the world renders an embedding; your Week-2 head scores it; you update with the same log-prob×advantage rule, subtracting a per-context baseline. If rm_head.npz from Week 2 exists it's loaded automatically — that's the swap-in, no code change.

context / prompt — one of the 4 synthetic "prompts" (your real pilot prompts later)
the steering knob the policy chooses, discretized to 9 grid values
reward from your Week-2 head scoring the rendered embedding
per-context baseline — the group-relative advantage of GRPO, one group per prompt
proxy: reward-model score true: your actual preference / quality
Grounds lec3–4 (policy gradients, RL implementation) for the loop itself, and the over-optimization / reward-hacking lecture for the right diagram: no KL leash is needed here (9 knob values, nowhere to collapse), but over-optimizing a noisy RM still bites where preference coverage is thin.
Do this — week3_alpha_policy/
  • README.md — the RLHF-mapping table and the swap-in
  • alpha_policy.py — 3 TODOs = Week-1 bandit + state-conditioned sampling + per-context baseline (solution: alpha_policy_solution.py)
  • Works out of the box: builds a ground-truth RM if Week 2's rm_head.npz is missing, else loads yours (same make_world(seed=0)).
  • Synthetic: each context's modal α lands on the grid point nearest the true α*; policy beats both uniform-random and a fixed hand-tuned α.
  • End-to-end: make_pairs.py --synthetic → reward_model_solution.py → alpha_policy.py runs with no external data.
  • You can point to where reward hacking would show up — the policy exploiting RM error at α values with little preference coverage.
04

Week 4 (optional) — one LLM-scale rep

One afternoon, then stop. Do a single DPO run on Qwen2.5-0.5B-Instruct with TRL's DPOTrainer on a free Colab T4 — a few thousand preference pairs, LoRA rank 8, ~30 min. The point is not a good model; it's seeing that the DPO loss from lec6 is the same shape as your Week-2 Bradley–Terry loss, at scale.

Same loss shape, one level up: BT ⟶ DPO

Week 2 fit where is a linear head on an embedding. DPO is the identical shape, but the "score" is now the log-ratio of your policy to a frozen reference model, scaled by . Recognizing this rhyme is the whole payoff of the week.

Grounds lec6 (DPO) and lec7 (synthetic data / modern post-training). Then eyeball chosen-vs-rejected logprob margins and stop — the goal was the recognition, not the checkpoint.
