How this course fits together

RLHF practitioners tend to think about the problem as one arc, not a list of independent topics: you start by deciding what to reward, you spend compute turning that reward into a policy update, and then you spend the rest of your time finding out where the reward signal lied to you.

Reward design → RL training → Reward hacking & evaluation

Reward design (what scalar are we even optimizing, and where does it come from) and reward hacking / evaluation (did optimizing that scalar actually make the model better, or just better at gaming the scalar) are the two ends of the arc that are genuinely hard to get right — most of the RL machinery in the middle is, in comparison, well-understood engineering. The tiers below reflect that: the topics that are hardest and most central to getting RLHF right are called out for depth; the RL training machinery is presented as infrastructure literacy — know the shape of it, not every derivation.

01

Core depth — go deep

Reward design and reward hacking are where RLHF is hardest to get right, because there is rarely a single correct answer and the failure modes are silent: a model can look like it's improving while it's actually learning to game the metric. These five pages are worth knowing cold.

Chapter 5

Reward Modeling

Bradley–Terry preference modeling, generative reward models / LLM-as-a-judge, ORM vs. PRM, and the reward-hacking taxonomy that follows from a miscalibrated reward.
Chapter 14 · the core objective spine

Over-Optimization & Reward Hacking

Goodhart's law in RLHF: the proxy reward climbs while the true objective rises, peaks, and falls. Proxy reward, the KL regularizer, and knowing when to stop optimizing.
Chapter 16

Evaluation

Why the instrument you trust to measure "did this help" is itself fragile: format sensitivity, log-likelihood scoring, contamination, and train-task reward vs. held-out generalization.
Lecture 2 · Chapters 4, 5 & 9

IFT, Reward Models & Rejection Sampling

Preference-data foundations: instruction tuning, the Bradley–Terry model from first principles, and curating data via rejection sampling before any RL loop exists.
Lecture 5 · Chapter 7

The Rise of Reasoning Models

RLVR — reinforcement learning with verifiable rewards — and why a checkable verifier is a fundamentally different (and easier) signal than a learned, gameable reward model.
02

RL-infra literacy — grasp the shape, not every derivation

Once the reward exists, turning it into a policy update is comparatively well-trodden engineering. You should know what GRPO's group-relative advantage and PPO's clipped objective are doing and why the KL penalty is there — you don't need to re-derive GAE or the clip bound from scratch to use these tools well.
