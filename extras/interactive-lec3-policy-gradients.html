Foundations — start here

Policy gradients sound advanced but rest on three school-level ideas: a gradient tells you which way is uphill, an expectation is just an average over the answers you sample, and variance is how noisy that average is. The clever tricks on this page (the log-derivative, baselines, RLOO) are all about getting a less-noisy estimate of the same uphill direction.

Gradients point uphill — and we want to climb

A slope is how steeply something changes; a gradient is the slope in every parameter direction at once, pointing the way the objective rises fastest. Supervised learning subtracts it to push loss down; RL adds it to push expected reward up — same compass, opposite goal.

the objective — expected reward, the hill we're climbing.
its gradient: the uphill direction. The step (not ) is the only difference from gradient descent.

Expectation: the objective is an average over samples

We can't try every possible response, so we sample a handful, score each, and average. That average is the quantity RL maximizes — and because it's an average, we can estimate it from samples instead of computing it exactly.

average over responses drawn from the policy. More samples → a steadier estimate.

Variance, and why a baseline tames it

Variance is how spread-out your samples are. A noisy reward estimate makes the gradient jitter and learning crawl. Subtracting a baseline (a typical reward) from each sample re-centers everything around zero: the average direction is unchanged, but the spread — and the jitter — shrinks.

the baseline — a reference reward subtracted from each sample. Choosing it well (e.g. RLOO uses the other samples' average) is the variance-reduction trick.
the centered reward (an advantage): positive = better than typical → push up, negative = worse → push down.

Language models as an episodic MDP

An RLHF rollout is a Markov decision process in disguise. The state is the prompt plus tokens generated so far, the action is the next token, the transition is deterministic (just append), and a single terminal reward scores the whole completion. Step through an episode and watch the state grow.

Step through an episode

One prompt → one completion is one episode. At each step the policy samples the next token, the state appends it (deterministic transition), and only at the very end does the reward model / verifier emit a single scalar . Discount — no per-step rewards.

Baselines reduce variance (the payoff)

Raw returns are noisy: the same action shows up in trajectories with wildly different totals. A baseline centers the signal — "was the return higher or lower than expected?" — and because doesn't depend on the sampled action, the expected gradient is unchanged, only the variance drops.

the baseline: a state-only reference value (often ) subtracted from the return
the centered learning signal — positive when this action beat the baseline, negative when worse
probabilities sum to one, so its gradient is zero — that's why subtracting adds no bias

KL as regularization

The reference policy is a frozen copy of the SFT checkpoint. The KL penalty pulls the trained policy back toward it, so the policy can't drift into reward-hacking territory. Don't confuse the two "old" policies: is the sampling policy (last rollout, used for the importance ratio); is the frozen SFT anchor (used for KL). They serve different jobs.
