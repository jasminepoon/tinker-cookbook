01

Language models as an episodic MDP

An RLHF rollout is a Markov decision process in disguise. The state s_t = (x, y_<t) is the prompt plus tokens generated so far, the action a_t is the next token, the transition is deterministic (just append), and a single terminal reward scores the whole completion. Step through an episode and watch the state grow.

Step through an episode

One prompt → one completion is one episode. At each step the policy π_θ(a_t | s_t) samples the next token, the state appends it (deterministic transition), and only at the very end does the reward model / verifier emit a single scalar R(τ). Discount γ = 1 — no per-step rewards.

02

The policy gradient equation

Every algorithm in this lecture is the same update with a different learning signal: ∇_θ J(θ) = E[ Σ_t ∇_θ log π_θ(a_t|s_t) · Ψ_t ]. The log-prob gradient answers which action?; the scalar Ψt answers how good was it? Multiply them per token and sum.

Per-token: ∇log π scaled by Ψt

Each completion token contributes one term ∇_θ log π_θ(a_t|s_t) · Ψ_t. Set a uniform learning signal Ψ for the whole sequence, or click a token to inspect its term. Positive Ψ pushes that token's probability up; negative pushes it down; the magnitude of ∇log π is larger for tokens the policy was uncertain about.

Bar height = |∇log π| ≈ how much room the token has to move (small for near-1 probabilities, large for uncertain ones). Bar color = sign of the update Ψ·∇log π. Click a token for its exact term.

03

The log-derivative (REINFORCE) trick

We want ∇_θ E_{τ~π_θ}[R(τ)], but the sampling distribution itself depends on θ, and ∇_θ p_θ(τ) is not a distribution we can sample from. The identity ∇_θ log p_θ = ∇_θ p_θ / p_θ rewrites the troublesome gradient as an expectation we can Monte-Carlo estimate.

Why ∇log π = ∇π / π lets us sample

Pick a token probability π and a parameter sensitivity ∇_θ π (how much π changes per unit θ). The identity says ∇_θ log π = ∇_θ π / π. Multiplying the integrand by p/p turns ∫ ∇p · R dτ (not samplable) into ∫ p · (∇log p · R) dτ = E[∇log p · R] (samplable). Watch both sides match.

The same gradient, two ways. Small π (rare action) magnifies the log-prob gradient — the 1/π factor is why low-probability sampled actions get amplified credit.

04

Baselines reduce variance (the payoff)

Raw returns are noisy: the same action shows up in trajectories with wildly different totals. A baseline b(s_t) centers the signal — "was the return higher or lower than expected?" — and because b doesn't depend on the sampled action, E[∇log π · b] = b · ∇Σ_a π = b · ∇1 = 0: the expected gradient is unchanged, only the variance drops.

Monte-Carlo estimator: with vs. without a baseline

A toy single-state policy over 4 actions. The true gradient is fixed; we estimate it by sampling actions and weighting g(a)·Ψ, where Ψ is either the raw return G or the centered return G − b (with b = mean return). Draw batches and watch the cloud of estimates: both center on the same true value (unbiased), but the baselined cloud is far tighter (lower variance).

RLOO: the leave-one-out baseline

Generate K completions for one prompt; for completion k use the mean of the other K−1 rewards as its baseline: b_k = (1/(K−1)) Σ_{i≠k} R_i, so Â_k = R_k − b_k. This per-prompt baseline captures prompt difficulty for free and is the bridge to GRPO. Drag the rewards.

The deck's worked example uses K=4 rewards [0.8, 0.3, 0.6, 0.5]. Best completion gets reinforced (Â>0), worst suppressed (Â<0). The advantages always sum to ~0 across the group.

06

KL as regularization

The reference policy π_ref is a frozen copy of the SFT checkpoint. The KL penalty −β·KL_t pulls the trained policy π_θ back toward it, so the policy can't drift into reward-hacking territory. Don't confuse the two "old" policies: π_old is the sampling policy (last rollout, used for the importance ratio); π_ref is the frozen SFT anchor (used for KL). They serve different jobs.

The β slider: KL penalty and the shaped objective

Per token, KL_t = log π_θ(a_t|s_t) − log π_ref(a_t|s_t). The shaped reward is r̃_t = −β·KL_t for intermediate tokens, and r̃_T = R(τ) − β·KL_T at the end. Drag β and the policy's drift, and watch the penalty eat into the reward.

In practice · This β is the single knob that controls reward over-optimization: push it too low and a high reward stops meaning high quality. The "Over-Optimization & Reward Hacking" page makes that failure curve explicit.
