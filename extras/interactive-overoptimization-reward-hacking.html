Over-Optimization & Reward Hacking

RL is a very strong optimizer — it pulls every drop of reward out of the environment. When the reward is only a proxy for what we actually want, that strength becomes the problem. The proxy keeps climbing while the true objective rises, peaks, and falls. This is Goodhart's law in RLHF, and learning to detect it and decide when to stop is the core of reward/environment QA.

The Objective Spine

Every RLHF run optimizes one thing: a learned proxy reward minus a KL leash back to the reference model. The whole over-optimization story lives in the tension between those two terms — push reward harder, and you drift further from , into regions where the proxy and the truth diverge.

This is the KL-regularized RLHF objective (Chapter 3). The reward model is a proxy — only correlated with true human preference. The KL penalty is a leash: a larger keeps the policy near its starting point; a smaller hands the optimizer freedom to chase reward (and to over-optimize). 03

The KL Penalty as a Brake

Over-optimization is fundamental — RLHF can never fully solve it — but it can be controlled. The chapter's key lever: with RL training, a higher KL penalty reduces over-optimization at a given KL distance, but it takes more training steps to get there. And different methods spend KL at different rates: online RL (PPO) spends far more KL than inference-time best-of-N.

Higher : less drift per step, more steps needed

A toy training trajectory: each step the optimizer takes a reward gradient , but the KL penalty pulls back proportionally to current drift, so the policy settles at a distance . Raise and the run lands at a smaller KL distance (less over-optimization, higher gold reward at convergence) — but needs more steps to get there. 04

How You Detect It: Train-RM vs Held-out-RM

The practical QA view (Bai et al. 2022). Split your preference data in half: train a reward model on one half, hold out the other as a test/gold RM. As training proceeds the train-RM score keeps improving — but past a point (~150K samples in the paper) the gains stop transferring to the held-out RM. That divergence is your alarm bell: the optimizer is hacking the train RM, not learning true quality.

The divergence you watch for

Drag the training progress slider. Early on, both the train RM and the held-out RM rise together — real improvement. Past the divergence point the train RM keeps climbing while the held-out RM stalls and then drops. The gap between the two curves is over-optimization made measurable. The shaded region marks where you're past the held-out peak — and should have stopped.

06

Mitigations

You can't eliminate over-optimization, but you can buy more reward per unit of KL and make the trade-off easier to manage. The chapter names several levers; click any row to expand. The interactive one — RM ensembles — shows why averaging several reward models is harder to hack.

RM ensembles: harder to hack than one RM

A single reward model has idiosyncratic blind spots the optimizer will find and exploit. Averaging K independently-trained RMs (Coste et al. 2023) cancels their independent errors: the ensemble's effective error shrinks like 1/√K, so the optimizer has less spurious signal to chase and the gold reward peaks later and higher. Drag K and watch the hackable "noise" in the proxy shrink.
