Reward Modeling

Reward models are where the messy, hard-to-specify human preference gets compressed into a single learnable objective. This page lets you build and break that objective — from the Bradley–Terry loss to LLM-as-a-judge, ORMs and PRMs, and the moment a biased grader starts handing out the wrong reward.

01

The Bradley–Terry Reward Model

The canonical RLHF reward model attaches a small linear head to a language model so it outputs one scalar score per prompt–completion pair. It is trained on pairs of chosen and rejected completions, and the loss depends only on the difference of their scores. This is the foundation; a basics page covers it in depth, so we keep it tight here.

Pairwise loss: −log σ(rc − rr)

Bradley–Terry models the probability the judge prefers the chosen completion as P(y_c ≻ y_r) = σ(r_c − r_r). Training minimizes −log σ(r_c − r_r). Only the gap matters — add a constant to both scores and nothing changes. Drag the two reward-head outputs and watch the preference probability and loss respond.

When rc > rr the model orders the pair correctly and loss is small. Flip them and loss shoots up — the gradient pushes the chosen score up and the rejected score down.

In practice · the entire skill of preference data is producing pairs whose ordering you actually trust — when there is no clean label, your (y_c, y_r) choice is the reward spec.
02

Generative Reward Modeling (LLM-as-a-Judge)

Instead of a scalar head, prompt a capable language model with a rubric, a question, and one or two responses, and let it grade. This is the grader work in textbook form — and it is where the reward signal quietly goes wrong. Below: pointwise vs pairwise judging, the two classic judge biases (position/order and self-preference), and how to measure grader consistency.

Pointwise judging against a rubric

A pointwise judge scores one response on weighted criteria (helpfulness, accuracy, depth, …) and aggregates to a scalar reward. Tune how much the judge cares about each criterion and how each response performs; the weighted score is the reward the policy will chase.

Weights are renormalized to sum to 1. Notice a response can score high overall while being inaccurate if the judge barely weights accuracy — that is a reward-spec bug, not a model bug.

Pairwise judging: position & self-preference bias

The MT-Bench prompt asks the judge to pick "[[A]]", "[[B]]", or "[[C]]" (tie) — and explicitly begs it to "avoid any position biases." It begs because judges have them. A real judge nudges its verdict toward whichever slot is shown first, and toward responses that look like its own. Set the true quality gap, then dial in each bias and see the verdict flip.

Verdict logit = Δ (true gap, favors B if >0) − position bias (favors A) − self-pref (favors whichever is the judge's family). The judge can prefer A even when B is genuinely better.

In practice · the cheapest, highest-leverage grader QA is averaging both A/B orderings — if the verdict isn't order-invariant, the bias is contaminating every reward.

Grader consistency: flip-rate & inter-rater agreement

A reward signal is only as good as it is reproducible. Two probes: the order-flip rate (how often swapping A/B flips the verdict — pure position bias) and inter-rater agreement across independent judge runs, summarized by Cohen's κ (chance-corrected: κ=1 perfect, κ=0 no better than coin-flip). Raise temperature or position bias and watch consistency collapse.

The chapter notes a common trick: judge at temperature 0 to cut rating variance. Slide temperature down to 0 and watch κ climb. But temperature can't fix a systematic position bias — that drives the flip-rate.

03

Outcome vs Process Reward Models

For reasoning tasks, where you put the supervision matters. An Outcome RM (ORM) judges a solution only by its final answer — correct steps with a wrong landing look bad, and a lucky wrong-step-but-right-answer looks fine. A Process RM (PRM) labels every reasoning step (+1 / 0 / −1), so it catches the broken step that ORM is blind to.

Same solution, two reward models

A 5-step solution to a math problem, with one intentionally wrong intermediate step that still happens to reach the right final answer. Toggle which reward model is scoring. ORM only sees the terminal correctness label, copied across all tokens; PRM scores each step at its boundary and flags the bad one. Edit which step is broken to see credit assignment move.

In practice · when the final label is cheap but step labels are expensive, you choose what to grade — and that choice decides whether the policy can learn to fix its reasoning or just its answer.
04

Reward-Model Variants

The base loss has been tweaked many ways without a single standard emerging. Two from the chapter: Llama 2's margin loss (use the strength of a preference, not just its direction), and InstructGPT's K-wise / multiple-comparisons-per-prompt reweighting (turn one prompt's ranking into all its pairs, averaged so correlated pairs don't overfit).

Preference margin loss

When annotators give Likert scores, the gap encodes how much better the chosen response is. Llama 2 subtracts a margin: −log σ(r_c − r_r − m), forcing the model to separate scores by at least m before the loss relaxes. Drag the margin and the score gap; watch the loss curve shift right.

m=0 recovers the plain BT loss (grey dashed). A larger m shifts the whole loss curve right: the model must build a bigger score gap before it's "satisfied." Llama 3 later dropped the margin as gains flattened with scale.

K-wise: comparisons per prompt

InstructGPT samples K completions per prompt and ranks them, yielding C(K,2) correlated pairs. Naively shuffling them lets prompts with more completions dominate the loss and invites overfitting; the fix is to average all C(K,2) pairs into one grouped update per prompt. Drag K and watch the pair count grow combinatorially while the per-pair weight shrinks to keep each prompt's contribution balanced.

Pairs per prompt = C(K,2) = K(K−1)/2. The grouped loss multiplies by 1/C(K,2) so a prompt with K=9 (36 pairs) doesn't outvote a prompt with K=2 (1 pair).

In practice · more completions per prompt is cheap signal — but only if you weight it so a few verbose prompts don't quietly become your whole reward model.
05

RM Quality → Training Signal

The reward model is a proxy objective: the policy optimizes whatever the RM rewards, faithfully including its mistakes. A biased or low-agreement RM doesn't just add noise — it adds a systematic error the policy will happily exploit. This is the on-ramp to reward over-optimization (the next page).

True reward vs. a biased proxy

Suppose true quality rises with answer correctness, but the RM has a length bias — it adds spurious reward for verbosity. The policy maximizes the proxy (orange), so it drifts toward whatever the proxy over-rewards. Add bias and noise and watch where the policy's optimum lands relative to the true optimum.

In practice · a grader that's biased or only weakly self-consistent doesn't fail loudly — it ships a confidently-wrong reward the optimizer will find and hack. Grader QA is the safety work.
