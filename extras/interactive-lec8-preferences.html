01

From messy wanting to a single number

The through-line of preference history — Port Royal (1662), Bentham, Ramsey, then von Neumann & Morgenstern (1947) — is the hope that human wanting collapses to one scalar. The VNM theorem is the mathematical permission slip RLHF leans on: "just fit a scalar reward." Bradley–Terry (1952) is the statistical bridge that turns pairwise comparisons into those scalars.

02

The VNM bait-and-switch

The utility theorem gives a scalar reward if preferences are complete, transitive, stable, and context-independent. In RLHF essentially none of those hold: preferences drift, they are framing-dependent, they can be intransitive at high complexity, and they are multidimensional — squashed into one number. Two panels make the breakage concrete.

Intransitivity: a preference cycle no scalar can fit

Transitivity says and . But real pairwise preferences can cycle like rock-paper-scissors: . No single scalar utility can represent a cycle — any numbering forces a total order, and a total order has no loops. One scalar, many things

A reward model compresses helpfulness, honesty, harmlessness, tone, format, length, plus the annotator's psychology and the interface framing — all into one number . We then optimize hard against that scalar and over-optimize whatever happened to get extra weight (often noise). 03

Preference data — the engine

It is far easier to judge than to generate, so we collect preferences — but how we collect them is the most opaque part of the pipeline, and every interface shapes the signal it captures. As of 2026 no open model ships fully open human preference data with its collection methodology.

Preference-data interface gallery

The deck shows five real collection interfaces. Each one captures a different signal from the same underlying judgment. Switch interfaces, interact with the widget, and watch exactly what label gets written to disk — and how much information is thrown away.

Rankings vs. ratings (Likert granularity)

A rating scores one completion (e.g. an 8-point Likert, early Claude; or 1–5). A ranking is a relative comparison. Almost everyone trains on pairwise rankings binarized to chosen/rejected for the Bradley–Terry loss, keeping ratings on the side. The Likert granularity (5-point with ties vs 8-point without) is itself a design choice that changes the data. Set each completion's Likert score and see how it binarizes into a chosen/rejected pair — and when it becomes a tie.

Bias: length & position distort the learned reward

Subtle systematic biases sail from the data into the model: verbosity (longer rated higher), position/order (the first-shown or prefix answer over-preferred), formatting, sycophancy, flattery. Here, two answers have equal true quality, but the annotator's observed preference is nudged by length and which side is shown first. The reward model fits the nudged label — so it learns to be long and to game position, not to be good.

the true quality gap between the two answers (the signal we actually want to learn)
verbosity-bias strength: how many reward points each extra token buys
position-bias strength nudging the verdict toward whichever answer is shown first (sign flips with the toggle)
the observed score gap the annotator actually acts on — true gap plus the two biases

In practice · This is the reward-hacking story one step upstream: the bias never has to enter the RL loop at all if it's already baked into the labels. Auditing preference data for length/position/sycophancy artifacts before training a reward model is cheaper than discovering the hack after the fact.
