Evaluation

Evaluation is the instrument you trust to know whether a reward change, a data change, or a training run actually helped. This page makes the fragility of that instrument tangible — formatting swings, log-likelihood scoring, contamination, generalization vs. training reward, and why two labs' leaderboard numbers are not comparable.

01

Prompting Format & Scoring

A single eval number hides a stack of choices: how many in-context examples, whether the model reasons before answering, and whether you score a generated string or compare log-probabilities. The chapter warns that formatting alone "can make a model's performance drop from 60% to near 0." These first panels let you feel that swing.

Prompt-formatting sensitivity

The same eval question, scored under different formats. Toggle few-shot vs zero-shot, chain-of-thought vs direct, and log-likelihood (multiple-choice) vs free-generation scoring, and watch the measured accuracy for one fixed model swing across the whole format grid. The model didn't change — only how you asked and graded.

In practice · One eval number is a point estimate over a format you happened to pick. Before you trust a delta, pin the format — or report the whole grid.

Log-likelihood (multiple-choice) scoring

For an MMLU-style question, one scoring route never samples at all: take each option's tokens, sum their conditional log-probabilities given the prompt, and pick the most likely option. But raw summed log-prob favors short answers. Drag each option's per-token log-prob and toggle length normalization to see how the predicted answer — and whether it's correct — flips.

Correct answer is (C) The Extreme Value Theorem — a longer string. Raw sum log-prob can punish it just for having more tokens; length normalization divides by token count.

In practice · Log-likelihood scoring is static (no sampling noise) and common in pretraining evals; the normalization choice silently decides the winner.

CoT / reasoning-era eval prompts

Modern MMLU isn't single-token sampling anymore. Tülu 3 switched it to long chain-of-thought with an exact-match answer regex: the model reasons, then must finish with "Therefore, the answer is (X)". Step through the answer string and watch the extractor try to recover the letter — and fail when the format suffix is missing.

In practice · Format-agnostic grading is rare and expensive. A correct answer the regex can't find scores as wrong — the eval measures formatting compliance, not capability.
02

What You're Actually Measuring

Even with the format pinned, the number can lie. Train/test overlap inflates it, and a metric that moves on the training task tells you nothing until a held-out eval moves too. This is the core of judgment instrumentation: trust the held-out instrument, not the training reward.

Contamination inflates the score

If a fraction of your test questions leaked into training (n-gram overlap from scraped web data, benchmark prompts in datasets), the model answers those near-perfectly for the wrong reason. Slide the contamination fraction and watch the apparent accuracy split away from the true held-out accuracy. The gap is pure illusion.

In practice · Decontaminate before you believe a jump. The most damning tell is a model that improves under RL on random rewards — only possible if the answer was already memorized.

Generalization, not training reward

The role-critical panel. You make a data/reward change and the training-task metric climbs. Do you ship it? Not until a held-out eval moves. Slide how much your change is real generalization vs. how much it just over-fits the training distribution, and watch the two curves diverge. The held-out curve is the only one you trust.

In practice · Training reward going up is necessary, never sufficient. The instrument you trust is the held-out eval; a flat held-out curve under a rising train curve means you memorized the reward, not the skill.

03

Comparing & Using Evals

Numbers from different press releases are not comparable: each lab fixes its own prompts, harness, and decoding, and none disclose them. Olmo 3 found held-constant eval setups still carry 0.25–1.5 point standard deviations — and bigger swings come from changing the harness. The last panels make harness drift reorder a leaderboard, and sketch how labs actually use evals.

Why external comparisons are unreliable

Three models are "ranked" by a benchmark, but each reported number was produced under a slightly different harness. Perturb the harness settings — prompt template, decoding temperature, answer-extraction strictness, few-shot count — within the small ranges the chapter documents, and watch the leaderboard reorder. Within a ±1.5 point noise band, the ranking is essentially a coin flip.

In practice · Treat a slightly-better external number as a tie. Reproducible comparison only exists inside your own controlled harness.

How labs use evals internally: statistical power

Labs hillclimb on a few private evals. The key thing better evals buy is statistical power: lower noise on your prioritized signal so you can tell a real training delta from chance. Set the true improvement between two runs and the per-eval noise, then add more eval items and watch the chance of correctly detecting the winner rise.

In practice · "Improving the eval" usually means shrinking its noise, not raising the bar — that's what lets you compare training runs with confidence instead of chasing ghosts.
